## Why measurement systems matter

Reliable measurement makes engineering, trade, science, medicine, manufacturing, and logistics possible. Shared standards reduce error and make work reproducible across people and places.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| SI/metric system | Global standard in science and much of industry |
| US customary units | Still widely used in American construction and commerce |
| Unit conversion | Necessary when working across systems |
| Precision and accuracy | Important for quality control and scientific reasoning |
| Tolerance | Real-world work depends on acceptable variation, not only exact numbers |

## The metric system (SI)

The International System of Units (SI) is built on seven base units from which all other units derive.

| Quantity | Base unit | Symbol |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Length | meter | m |
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Time | second | s |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Electric current | ampere | A |
| Amount of substance | mole | mol |
| Luminous intensity | candela | cd |

The metric system uses a consistent set of prefixes for scaling: kilo- (1,000), centi- (1/100), milli- (1/1,000), micro- (1/1,000,000), and so on. This makes unit conversion within the system straightforward -- just move the decimal point.

<Alert tone="info" title="Why metric dominates science">
The prefix system and base-10 structure eliminate most conversion errors. Every country in the world has officially adopted SI, though the US, Liberia, and Myanmar still use non-metric units in daily life.
</Alert>

## US customary units

The United States uses a system descended from English units. It remains dominant in construction, cooking, road signage, and everyday commerce.

| Measurement | Common units | Metric equivalent |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Length | inch, foot, yard, mile | 1 inch = 2.54 cm, 1 mile = 1.609 km |
| Weight | ounce, pound, ton | 1 lb = 0.4536 kg |
| Volume | teaspoon, cup, gallon | 1 gallon = 3.785 L |
| Temperature | Fahrenheit | °F = (°C x 9/5) + 32 |

### When systems collide

Mixed-unit errors have caused real engineering failures. In 1999, NASA lost the Mars Climate Orbiter because one team used pound-force seconds while another used newton-seconds. The spacecraft entered the atmosphere at the wrong altitude and was destroyed.

## Precision vs accuracy

These terms are related but distinct.

- **Accuracy** means how close a measurement is to the true value.
- **Precision** means how repeatable a measurement is -- how close repeated measurements are to each other.

A tool can be precise but inaccurate (consistently wrong by the same amount) or accurate but imprecise (right on average but scattered). Good measurement requires both.

### Significant figures

Significant figures communicate the precision of a measurement. Reporting a length as 12.0 cm (three significant figures) implies more precision than 12 cm (two significant figures). In calculations, the result should not claim more precision than the least precise input.

## Tolerance in practical work

In manufacturing and construction, no measurement is perfectly exact. **Tolerance** defines the acceptable range of variation.

- A machined part specified as 25.00 mm ± 0.05 mm must fall between 24.95 mm and 25.05 mm.
- Tighter tolerances cost more because they require better tooling and more careful inspection.
- Understanding tolerance prevents over-specifying (wasting money) or under-specifying (causing failures).

<Alert tone="warning" title="Common mistake">
Using a ruler marked in 1/16-inch increments and reporting a measurement to the nearest thousandth of an inch is false precision. The tool's resolution limits the precision you can honestly claim.
</Alert>

## Practical study approach

1. Memorize the SI base units and the most common prefixes (kilo, centi, milli, micro).
2. Learn the key US-to-metric conversions for length, weight, volume, and temperature.
3. Practice estimating: know that a meter is roughly a yard, a kilogram is roughly 2.2 pounds, and a liter is roughly a quart.
4. Understand significant figures well enough to avoid false precision in your own work.
5. When working on any project, confirm which measurement system is expected before starting -- never assume.
