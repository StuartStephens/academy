## Core context

After the Civil War, the United States faced the challenge of rebuilding the South, defining citizenship, and determining whether formerly enslaved people would receive meaningful political and civil rights.

## Major themes

| Theme | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Citizenship and rights | Constitutional amendments redefined freedom and legal status |
| Federal enforcement | National power was used unevenly to protect civil rights |
| White supremacist backlash | Violence and political resistance undermined reform |
| Segregation | Jim Crow laws institutionalized racial inequality |

## Key turning points

1. Emancipation and constitutional amendments.
2. Federal reconstruction governments in the South.
3. Violent resistance and suppression of Black political power.
4. End of Reconstruction compromise and withdrawal of support.
5. Expansion of segregation and disenfranchisement.

## Long-term impact

Reconstruction briefly expanded democracy but its rollback shaped generations of racial inequality, setting the stage for the Civil Rights Movement.
