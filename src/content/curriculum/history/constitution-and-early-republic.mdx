## Core context

After independence, the United States still had to define how power would be organized. The Constitution created a stronger federal framework than the Articles of Confederation and shaped how law, rights, and institutions would operate.

## Major themes

| Theme | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Federalism | Power was divided between national and state governments |
| Separation of powers | Legislative, executive, and judicial branches were designed to limit each other |
| Rights and liberties | The Bill of Rights shaped debates over government power and individual protections |
| Political development | Early parties and competing visions emerged quickly |

## Key turning points

1. Weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation.
2. Constitutional Convention and ratification debates.
3. Addition of the Bill of Rights.
4. Rise of early party conflict.
5. Expansion of federal institutions and executive authority.

## Long-term impact

The Constitution became the core legal framework of the United States, but it also left major issues unresolved, especially around slavery, citizenship, and political inclusion.
