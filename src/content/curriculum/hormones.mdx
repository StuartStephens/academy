## What are hormones?

Hormones are chemical messengers that travel through the body and help regulate processes like metabolism, growth, stress response, sleep, mood, and reproduction. They are central to endocrine system function.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Endocrine glands | Different glands produce different signaling molecules |
| Hormone signaling | Hormones influence target tissues through receptors |
| Feedback loops | Many hormones are regulated through negative feedback |
| Growth and metabolism | Hormones shape energy use, tissue development, and repair |
| Reproduction and stress | Hormones affect fertility, adaptation, and survival response |

## How hormonal signaling works

Endocrine glands release hormones directly into the bloodstream. These hormones travel throughout the body but only affect cells that have the matching receptor -- like a key fitting a specific lock.

### Signal types

- **Endocrine signaling** -- hormones travel through blood to distant target cells. This is the classic hormone pathway (e.g., thyroid hormone affecting cells throughout the body).
- **Paracrine signaling** -- chemical messengers act on nearby cells without entering the bloodstream.
- **Autocrine signaling** -- a cell signals itself, often in immune and growth contexts.

The speed of hormonal effects varies widely. Adrenaline acts in seconds. Thyroid hormones take days to fully shift metabolic rate. Growth hormone effects unfold over months and years.

## Major glands and their hormones

| Gland | Location | Key hormones | Primary functions |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hypothalamus | Brain | Releasing/inhibiting hormones | Master regulator; controls pituitary |
| Pituitary | Base of brain | GH, TSH, ACTH, LH, FSH, prolactin | "Master gland"; directs other endocrine glands |
| Thyroid | Neck | T3, T4, calcitonin | Metabolic rate, energy, calcium regulation |
| Parathyroid | Behind thyroid | PTH | Calcium and phosphorus balance |
| Adrenal | Above kidneys | Cortisol, aldosterone, adrenaline | Stress response, blood pressure, electrolytes |
| Pancreas | Abdomen | Insulin, glucagon | Blood sugar regulation |
| Ovaries | Pelvis (female) | Estrogen, progesterone | Reproductive cycle, bone density, secondary sex characteristics |
| Testes | Scrotum (male) | Testosterone | Muscle mass, bone density, reproductive function |
| Pineal | Brain | Melatonin | Sleep-wake cycle regulation |

<Alert tone="info" title="The hypothalamic-pituitary axis">
The hypothalamus and pituitary gland form a control hierarchy. The hypothalamus senses conditions and releases hormones that tell the pituitary what to do. The pituitary then signals downstream glands (thyroid, adrenals, gonads). This two-tier system allows precise regulation.
</Alert>

## Feedback loops

Most hormones are regulated by **negative feedback** -- when hormone levels rise high enough, the signal to produce more is suppressed.

### Example: thyroid regulation

1. The hypothalamus releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone).
2. TRH stimulates the pituitary to release TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone).
3. TSH tells the thyroid to produce T3 and T4.
4. When T3/T4 levels are sufficient, they inhibit further TRH and TSH release.
5. If T3/T4 drop, the inhibition lifts and the cycle restarts.

This loop keeps thyroid hormone levels within a narrow range. Disruption at any point (autoimmune attack on the thyroid, pituitary tumor, iodine deficiency) can cause hypo- or hyperthyroidism.

## Key hormones in detail

### Insulin and glucagon

These two pancreatic hormones regulate blood sugar in opposition:

- **Insulin** -- released when blood sugar rises (after eating). It signals cells to absorb glucose and stores excess as glycogen and fat. Insulin deficiency or resistance causes diabetes.
- **Glucagon** -- released when blood sugar drops (during fasting). It signals the liver to release stored glycogen as glucose.

### Cortisol

The primary stress hormone, produced by the adrenal cortex:

- Raises blood sugar for energy availability
- Suppresses non-essential functions (immune response, digestion) during acute stress
- Follows a diurnal rhythm: highest in the morning, lowest at night
- Chronic elevation (from sustained stress, Cushing's syndrome, or exogenous steroids) causes weight gain, immune suppression, muscle loss, and bone weakening

### Testosterone and estrogen

Both hormones exist in all sexes but in different ratios:

- **Testosterone** -- primary androgen. Drives muscle development, bone density, body hair, and male reproductive function. Also affects mood, energy, and libido in all sexes.
- **Estrogen** -- primary female sex hormone (mainly estradiol). Regulates the menstrual cycle, supports bone density, affects cardiovascular health, and influences mood.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Hormone supplementation">
Exogenous hormones (testosterone replacement, estrogen therapy, anabolic steroids) can suppress natural production through feedback loops. Long-term use without medical supervision can cause serious health consequences. Always work with a qualified physician.
</Alert>

## Common endocrine disorders

| Condition | Mechanism | Key symptoms |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Type 1 diabetes | Autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing beta cells | High blood sugar, weight loss, fatigue |
| Type 2 diabetes | Insulin resistance, eventual beta cell burnout | High blood sugar, often linked to obesity |
| Hypothyroidism | Insufficient thyroid hormone production | Fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance |
| Hyperthyroidism | Excess thyroid hormone (often Graves' disease) | Weight loss, anxiety, heat intolerance, rapid heart rate |
| Addison's disease | Adrenal insufficiency (low cortisol) | Fatigue, low blood pressure, darkened skin |
| Cushing's syndrome | Excess cortisol | Weight gain (central), thin skin, high blood sugar |
| PCOS | Androgen excess in females, insulin resistance | Irregular periods, acne, excess hair growth |

## Practical study approach

1. Learn the major glands, their locations, and their key hormones -- the table above is a good starting framework.
2. Understand negative feedback using the thyroid axis as a model, then apply the pattern to other systems.
3. Study insulin and glucagon together to understand blood sugar regulation -- this is the most practically relevant hormone pair for daily health.
4. Explore one endocrine disorder in depth to see how disrupted signaling cascades through the body.
5. Recognize that hormones interact -- stress hormones affect sex hormones, thyroid hormones affect metabolism which affects insulin sensitivity, and so on. Isolated study of one hormone misses the system.
