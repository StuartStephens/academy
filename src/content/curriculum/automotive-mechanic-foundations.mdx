## Role overview

An automotive mechanic diagnoses, repairs, and maintains vehicles to keep them safe, reliable, and efficient.

The job combines mechanical understanding, electrical diagnostics, tool discipline, and clear communication with customers.

## Core systems every beginner should learn

| System | What to learn first |
| --- | --- |
| Engine | Combustion cycle, timing, compression basics |
| Braking | Hydraulic systems, pads/rotors, bleeding workflow |
| Suspension/steering | Alignment fundamentals, wear patterns |
| Electrical | Battery, charging, starter circuits, multimeter use |
| Cooling | Radiator flow, thermostat behavior, leak testing |
| Transmission | Manual vs automatic, fluid service, shift behavior |
| Fuel system | Injection, fuel pump, filter service, pressure testing |

## Essential workflow

1. Verify customer concern and reproduce symptom.
2. Inspect visually and run a structured diagnostic process.
3. Confirm root cause before replacing parts.
4. Repair with manufacturer specs and torque values.
5. Test drive and quality-check before delivery.

<Alert tone="info" title="Best early habit">
Do not guess-replace parts. Diagnose first, then repair with evidence.
</Alert>

### Why groups matter for mechanics

- **Shared platforms**: VW Group vehicles (VW Golf, Audi A3, Skoda Octavia) often share the same MQB platform. Suspension, steering, and drivetrain components can be nearly identical across brands.
- **Common diagnostic tools**: GM vehicles use GDS2/Tech2, Stellantis uses wiTECH, VW Group uses ODIS/VCDS. Knowing the corporate group tells you which scan tool you need.
- **Parts interchangeability**: Brands within a group frequently share engines, transmissions, and electrical architectures. A part number search under one brand may solve a sourcing issue for another.
- **Service procedure similarity**: If you learn Toyota service methodology, Lexus procedures will feel familiar. Stellantis brands share many service bulletins.

<Alert tone="info" title="Consolidation trend">
The automotive industry has consolidated heavily. Many brands that appear independent share engineering with a parent group. Geely (China) owning Volvo, and Tata (India) owning Jaguar Land Rover are examples that surprise many people.
</Alert>

### The EV shift

Electric vehicles are restructuring the industry. Key differences for mechanics:

- **Fewer moving parts**: no engine, transmission, or exhaust system. Brake wear is reduced by regenerative braking.
- **High-voltage systems**: EV drivetrains operate at 400-800V. Specialized safety training and insulated tools are mandatory.
- **Software-heavy**: EVs rely more on software updates, battery management systems, and integrated electronics.
- **New players**: Tesla, BYD, Rivian, and Lucid have no legacy dealership/service networks. Independent shops that invest in EV training will fill this gap.

<Alert tone="warning" title="EV safety">
High-voltage battery packs are lethal if mishandled. Never work on an EV high-voltage system without proper isolation procedures, PPE, and training. Orange-colored cables indicate high voltage.
</Alert>

## Entry path

- Start with a vocational program, apprenticeship, or entry-level shop role.
- Build confidence with maintenance services (oil, brakes, fluids, inspections).
- Learn scan tools and service manuals early.
- Pursue certifications that match your local market.
- Consider EV-specific training early -- demand for qualified EV technicians is growing rapidly.

## Shop professionalism

- Keep tools organized and calibrated.
- Follow safety standards for lifting, PPE, and chemical handling.
- Document diagnostics and work completed clearly.
- Stay current with technical service bulletins and recalls for the brands you service.

## Practical study approach

1. Learn the major vehicle systems and how they interact -- start with engine, braking, and electrical.
2. Memorize which brands belong to which groups so you can anticipate shared components and procedures.
3. Get comfortable with a multimeter and basic scan tool before attempting advanced diagnostics.
4. Practice on maintenance tasks (oil changes, brake jobs, fluid flushes) to build hand skill and confidence.
5. Study at least one EV platform to understand how the service model differs from ICE vehicles.
