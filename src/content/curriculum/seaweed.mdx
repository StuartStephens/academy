## Why seaweed matters

Seaweed is important in ecology, food systems, aquaculture, fertilizer use, industrial processing, and coastal economies. It is not a single plant type but a broad group of marine algae with very different uses and growth habits.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Marine ecology | Seaweed supports habitat, nutrient cycling, and coastal ecosystems |
| Food use | Many seaweeds are important in cooking and nutrition |
| Aquaculture | Seaweed farming can be economically and environmentally significant |
| Processing | Drying, extraction, and preservation affect commercial value |
| Species differences | Kelp, nori, wakame, and other types behave differently |

## Major groups

Seaweeds are classified by pigment into three broad groups, each with distinct habitats and uses.

| Group | Color | Examples | Typical habitat |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Chlorophyta | Green | Sea lettuce (Ulva), Codium | Shallow, well-lit water |
| Phaeophyta | Brown | Kelp (Laminaria), wakame, Sargassum | Temperate, cold, deeper water |
| Rhodophyta | Red | Nori (Porphyra), dulse, Irish moss | Intertidal to deep, warm and cold |

Brown algae include the largest species -- giant kelp can grow over 45 meters and form underwater forests that support entire ecosystems.

<Alert tone="info" title="Not actually plants">
Seaweeds are algae, not true plants. They lack roots, stems, and leaves. What looks like a root (holdfast) anchors them; what looks like a stem (stipe) provides structure; and what looks like a leaf (blade or frond) photosynthesizes.
</Alert>

## Seaweed as food

Seaweed is a staple ingredient in East Asian cuisines and increasingly used worldwide.

| Seaweed | Common use | Nutrition highlights |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nori | Sushi wraps, snack sheets | High protein for a seaweed, rich in iodine and B12 |
| Wakame | Miso soup, salads | Good source of fucoxanthin, calcium, magnesium |
| Kombu | Dashi stock, umami base | Very high in iodine, natural glutamate source |
| Dulse | Snack, seasoning, salads | High protein, smoky flavor, iron-rich |
| Sea lettuce | Salads, soups | Mild flavor, high iron and vitamin C |

Dried seaweed is shelf-stable and nutrient-dense relative to its weight, making it practical for storage and transport.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Iodine caution">
Some seaweeds -- especially kombu -- contain very high levels of iodine. Excessive iodine intake can disrupt thyroid function. Moderate consumption and vary the types you eat.
</Alert>

## Aquaculture and harvesting

Seaweed farming is one of the fastest-growing sectors of aquaculture globally. East and Southeast Asia dominate production, but operations are expanding in Europe, North America, and Africa.

### Farming methods

- **Rope or line cultivation** -- seeded ropes are suspended in coastal water. The most common commercial method.
- **Net cultivation** -- used for nori; nets are seeded with spores and placed in shallow bays.
- **Wild harvesting** -- hand-cutting from natural beds, still practiced for some species but limited by sustainability concerns.

Seaweed farming requires no freshwater, no feed, and no fertilizer. It absorbs dissolved nutrients (including excess nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff), making it a potential tool for coastal water quality improvement.

## Ecological role

- **Habitat**: kelp forests and seaweed beds provide shelter, food, and nursery areas for fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals.
- **Carbon capture**: seaweed absorbs CO2 during photosynthesis. Large-scale cultivation is being studied as a climate mitigation tool.
- **Nutrient cycling**: seaweed takes up nitrogen and phosphorus, helping balance coastal nutrient loads.
- **Coastal protection**: dense seaweed beds reduce wave energy and help stabilize shorelines.

## Industrial and commercial uses

Beyond food, seaweed-derived compounds are used in many industries:

- **Agar** (from red algae) -- gelling agent in microbiology, food, and cosmetics.
- **Carrageenan** (from red algae) -- thickener in dairy, processed foods, and pharmaceuticals.
- **Alginate** (from brown algae) -- used in wound dressings, food thickening, textile printing, and dental molds.
- **Fertilizer and soil conditioner** -- seaweed extracts improve soil structure and provide trace minerals.
- **Biofuel research** -- seaweed biomass is being explored as a feedstock for renewable fuels.

## Practical study approach

1. Learn the three major groups (green, brown, red) and how they differ in habitat, size, and use.
2. Try identifying and tasting at least three edible seaweeds: nori, wakame, and kombu are widely available.
3. Understand the basics of rope-line cultivation and why seaweed farming has low environmental impact.
4. Study one ecological function in depth -- kelp forest ecology is well-documented and visually compelling.
5. Explore one industrial application (agar, carrageenan, or alginate) to see how seaweed chemistry connects to everyday products.
