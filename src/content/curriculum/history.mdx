## Why study history?

Wars, revolutions, state-building, economic change, scientific achievement, and social movements all reshape institutions and public life. Studying them helps explain why modern societies, governments, and global systems work the way they do.

## What this section covers

This history curriculum starts with turning points that strongly shaped the United States and the modern world.

| Period | Core focus |
| --- | --- |
| Renaissance | Cultural, artistic, and intellectual transformation in early modern Europe |
| French Revolution | Popular sovereignty, revolution, and modern political change |
| Russian Revolution | Imperial collapse, revolution, and Soviet state formation |
| American Revolution | Independence, colonial resistance, founding government |
| Constitution and Early Republic | Federal institutions, rights, and early US political order |
| Westward Expansion | Territorial growth, indigenous dispossession, and political conflict |
| Civil War | Union, slavery, secession, reconstruction consequences |
| Reconstruction and Jim Crow | Citizenship, backlash, segregation, and inequality |
| Industrial Revolution | Mechanization, labor change, and industrial society |
| Great Depression and New Deal | Economic collapse and federal transformation |
| World War I | Industrial warfare, alliances, collapse of empires |
| World War II | Global total war, fascism, genocide, postwar order |
| Cold War | US-Soviet rivalry, deterrence, proxy conflict |
| Korean War | Division, intervention, armistice politics |
| Vietnam War | Containment, insurgency, political legitimacy, public opinion |
| Civil Rights Movement | Protest, law, voting rights, and equality struggles |
| Moon Landing and Space Race | Scientific competition and Cold War prestige |
| September 11 and War on Terror | Security-state expansion and global intervention |
| Afghanistan War | Counterinsurgency, occupation, and intervention limits |
| Iraq War | Regime change, occupation, and regional instability |
| Libya Intervention | Humanitarian intervention and post-conflict fragmentation |

## How to read these topics

1. Start with causes.
2. Understand major actors and alliances.
3. Track turning points and outcomes.
4. Study long-term consequences, not just battlefield events.

<Alert tone="info" title="History study tip">
Do not reduce history to dates and battles alone. The most important lessons often come from economics, ideology, logistics, law, technology, and political decision-making.
</Alert>
