## Why baking matters

Baking is the most precise branch of cooking. Unlike stovetop work where you can adjust in real time, baking commits you to ratios and timing before the oven door closes. Understanding the science produces reliable, repeatable results.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Flour | Protein content determines structure and texture |
| Leavening | Yeast and chemical leaveners create rise and crumb |
| Hydration | Water ratio controls dough texture and crumb openness |
| Fermentation | Time and temperature develop flavor and structure |
| Oven behavior | Heat transfer, steam, and timing shape the final product |

## Flour

Flour provides the structure of baked goods through gluten -- a protein network formed when wheat flour meets water and is worked mechanically.

| Flour type | Protein content | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bread flour | 12-14% | Chewy breads, pizza, bagels |
| All-purpose | 10-12% | General baking, cookies, muffins, simple breads |
| Cake flour | 7-9% | Tender cakes, delicate pastries |
| Whole wheat | 13-14% | Denser, nuttier breads (often blended with white flour) |
| Rye | Low gluten | Dark breads, sourdough blends (needs wheat flour for structure) |

Higher protein means more gluten development, which means chewier, stronger structure. Lower protein means tenderness.

## Leavening

### Yeast

Yeast is a living organism that consumes sugars and produces CO2 gas (which makes dough rise) and alcohol (which contributes flavor and evaporates during baking).

- **Active dry yeast**: needs to be dissolved in warm water before use.
- **Instant yeast**: can be mixed directly into dry ingredients. Faster and more convenient.
- **Fresh / cake yeast**: perishable, used in professional bakeries. Strongest flavor contribution.

Yeast is sensitive to temperature. Below 70°F it works slowly; at 75-80°F it is most active; above 120°F it dies.

### Chemical leaveners

- **Baking soda** (sodium bicarbonate): reacts with acids (buttermilk, yogurt, vinegar, brown sugar) to produce CO2. Acts immediately -- mix and bake promptly.
- **Baking powder**: baking soda pre-mixed with a dry acid. Double-acting versions react once when wet and again when heated.

<Alert tone="info" title="When to use which">
Use baking soda when the recipe has an acidic ingredient. Use baking powder when it does not. Using the wrong one produces off-flavors (metallic or soapy) and poor rise.
</Alert>

## Baker's percentages

Professional bakers express recipes as percentages relative to the total flour weight. Flour is always 100%.

| Ingredient | Baker's % (basic bread) |
| --- | --- |
| Flour | 100% |
| Water | 65-75% |
| Salt | 2% |
| Yeast | 1-2% |

This system makes it easy to scale recipes up or down and to compare hydration across different breads. A 65% hydration dough is stiffer and easier to shape; a 75%+ hydration dough is wetter, harder to handle, but produces a more open crumb.

## Basic bread process

### 1. Mixing

Combine flour, water, salt, and yeast. Mix until a shaggy dough forms. Autolyse (resting the flour and water for 20-30 minutes before adding salt and yeast) hydrates the flour and starts gluten development with minimal effort.

### 2. Kneading / folding

Develop gluten through kneading (10-12 minutes by hand) or stretch-and-fold technique (a series of folds at intervals during bulk fermentation). The dough should become smooth, elastic, and pass the windowpane test (you can stretch a small piece thin enough to see light through without it tearing).

### 3. Bulk fermentation

The first rise. Dough ferments at room temperature (or in the fridge for a cold retard) until roughly doubled. During this stage, yeast produces gas, acids develop flavor, and gluten matures.

### 4. Shaping

Divide the dough, pre-shape into a loose round, rest 15-20 minutes, then final shape (boule, batard, or loaf pan). Good shaping creates surface tension, which controls oven spring and final form.

### 5. Proofing

The second rise after shaping. Can be done at room temp (1-2 hours) or retarded overnight in the fridge for deeper flavor. The dough is ready when it springs back slowly when poked.

### 6. Baking

- **High initial heat** (450-500°F) with steam drives oven spring -- the final burst of rise in the first 10-15 minutes.
- **Steam** keeps the crust flexible during oven spring. Professional ovens inject steam; at home, use a Dutch oven (lid on) or a pan of water on the oven floor.
- **Drop temperature** after 15-20 minutes and bake until the crust is deep golden and the internal temperature reaches 200-210°F.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Patience at the end">
Bread must cool completely on a wire rack before slicing. The interior continues cooking and setting as it cools. Cutting too early compresses the crumb and releases steam that would otherwise be reabsorbed.
</Alert>

## Sourdough

Sourdough replaces commercial yeast with a natural leaven (starter) -- a culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria maintained by regular feeding of flour and water.

- **Flavor**: sourdough produces complex, tangy flavors that commercial yeast cannot replicate.
- **Maintenance**: a starter must be fed regularly (daily at room temp, or weekly if refrigerated).
- **Timing**: sourdough fermentation is slower and more temperature-sensitive. Bulk ferment can take 4-12 hours depending on starter strength and ambient temperature.

## Practical study approach

1. Bake a simple no-knead bread to understand hydration, fermentation, and oven behavior with minimal technique.
2. Learn baker's percentages and use them to read and compare recipes.
3. Practice shaping until you can create consistent surface tension.
4. Bake the same recipe three times, changing one variable each time (hydration, proof time, oven temp) to see the effect.
5. Start a sourdough starter when you are ready for a long-term project -- it teaches fermentation intuition.
