## Why vegetable cooking matters

Vegetables are the most undercooked and over-boiled category in home kitchens. Proper technique transforms them from an obligation into the best part of a meal. The difference between limp steamed broccoli and properly roasted broccoli with crisp edges is entirely technique.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Dry-heat methods | Roasting, grilling, and sautéing develop flavor through browning |
| Wet-heat methods | Blanching and steaming preserve color and snap |
| Seasoning | Vegetables need salt, fat, acid, and heat just like protein does |
| Doneness | Undercooked is raw and tough; overcooked is mushy and dull |
| Vegetable categories | Root, cruciferous, leafy, and allium vegetables cook differently |

## Roasting

Roasting is the single most effective technique for making vegetables taste good. High heat caramelizes natural sugars and creates browned, crispy edges.

### Process

1. Cut vegetables into even pieces (1-inch chunks for most).
2. Toss with oil, salt, and pepper. Use enough oil to coat -- vegetables need fat to brown.
3. Spread in a single layer on a sheet pan. **Do not crowd.** Crowding traps steam and prevents browning.
4. Roast at 425-450°F, stirring once halfway through.
5. Done when edges are browned and caramelized, interior is tender.

| Vegetable | Roasting time at 425°F | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Broccoli | 18-22 min | Cut into florets, include some stem |
| Brussels sprouts | 20-25 min | Halve for flat browning surface |
| Carrots | 25-30 min | Cut into even sticks or coins |
| Sweet potatoes | 25-30 min | 1-inch cubes |
| Cauliflower | 25-30 min | Florets or thick slabs |
| Zucchini | 15-18 min | Half-moons, high heat to avoid sogginess |
| Asparagus | 10-12 min | Trim woody ends, keep whole |

<Alert tone="info" title="The crowding mistake">
The most common roasting error is overcrowding the pan. When vegetables are piled up, they steam in their own moisture instead of browning. Use two pans if needed, or roast in batches.
</Alert>

## Sautéing

Sautéing means cooking quickly in a small amount of fat over high heat, with frequent movement.

### Process

1. Heat the pan first, then add oil.
2. Add vegetables when the oil shimmers.
3. Keep the vegetables moving -- toss or stir frequently.
4. Cook until tender-crisp with some browning.
5. Season with salt during cooking, finish with acid (lemon juice, vinegar) or fresh herbs.

Best for: green beans, snap peas, mushrooms, spinach, zucchini, bell peppers.

Sautéing works best with smaller or thinner-cut pieces that cook quickly. For denser vegetables (carrots, broccoli), blanch first, then sauté to finish.

## Blanching

Blanching is briefly boiling vegetables and then immediately shocking them in ice water. It sets vibrant color, removes raw bite, and stops cooking precisely.

### Process

1. Bring a large pot of heavily salted water to a rolling boil.
2. Add vegetables and cook for 1-3 minutes (varies by vegetable and size).
3. Transfer immediately to an ice bath.
4. Drain when fully cooled.

Blanching is the standard professional method for green vegetables that will be served later (reheated by sauté, added to salads, or plated cold). It is also the first step before freezing vegetables for storage.

## Steaming

Steaming cooks vegetables gently with water vapor. It preserves nutrients and texture but does not develop browning or caramelization.

- Use a steamer basket over boiling water, lid on.
- Steam until just tender -- start checking early.
- Season after steaming (steaming alone does not add flavor).

Steaming is best for delicate vegetables (fish-adjacent preparations, baby vegetables) or when you want clean flavors without added fat.

## Seasoning vegetables

Vegetables need seasoning just as aggressively as protein.

### The four elements

- **Salt**: season during cooking, not just at the end. Salt draws out moisture and concentrates flavor.
- **Fat**: oil, butter, or rendered drippings. Fat carries flavor compounds and enables browning.
- **Acid**: lemon juice, vinegar, or pickled elements added at the end. Acid brightens flat-tasting vegetables.
- **Heat (spice)**: chili flakes, black pepper, or fresh chili. Adds dimension.

### Common finishing additions

- Parmesan cheese (umami boost)
- Toasted nuts or seeds (texture contrast)
- Fresh herbs (brightness -- parsley, dill, cilantro, mint)
- Garlic (add late in cooking to avoid burning)

<Alert tone="warning" title="Raw garlic burns fast">
Minced garlic burns in 30-60 seconds over high heat, turning bitter. Add it in the last minute of cooking, or use whole crushed cloves that can be removed. Burned garlic ruins a dish.
</Alert>

## Vegetable categories

| Category | Examples | Key cooking considerations |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Root vegetables | Carrot, beet, parsnip, turnip | Dense; need longer cook times, roast well |
| Cruciferous | Broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, cabbage | Excellent roasted; avoid boiling to mush |
| Alliums | Onion, garlic, shallot, leek | Build flavor base; caramelize beautifully |
| Leafy greens | Spinach, kale, chard, arugula | Cook quickly; wilt dramatically in volume |
| Nightshades | Tomato, eggplant, bell pepper | Versatile; roast, grill, sauté, or eat raw |
| Squash | Zucchini, butternut, acorn, delicata | Summer squash is quick; winter squash needs roasting |
| Legumes (fresh) | Green beans, snap peas, edamame | Blanch or sauté; stay crisp-tender |

## Practical study approach

1. Roast a sheet pan of broccoli or Brussels sprouts at 425°F with proper spacing -- this single exercise teaches more about vegetable cooking than any other.
2. Practice blanching green beans until you can consistently get bright green, snappy results.
3. Sauté mushrooms without crowding the pan -- they should brown, not steam.
4. Add acid (lemon or vinegar) to a finished vegetable dish and notice the difference.
5. Cook the same vegetable three ways (roasted, sautéed, steamed) and compare flavor and texture.
