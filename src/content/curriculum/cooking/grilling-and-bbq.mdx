## Why grilling matters

Grilling is one of the most versatile and accessible cooking methods. It produces flavors that cannot be replicated indoors -- caramelization from radiant heat, smoke from dripping fat, and the char that comes from open flame.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Heat types | Direct vs indirect heat determines what you can cook |
| Fuel source | Charcoal, gas, and wood produce different flavors and heat profiles |
| Fire management | Controlling temperature zones is the primary skill |
| Smoking | Low-and-slow cooking transforms tough cuts into tender meals |
| Safety | Grills are open flames with hot grease; respect the hazards |

## Direct vs indirect heat

This is the most important concept in grilling.

- **Direct heat**: food is placed directly over the fire. Used for thin, quick-cooking items -- steaks, burgers, vegetables, fish fillets. High heat, short time.
- **Indirect heat**: food is placed away from the fire, with the lid closed. The grill acts as an oven. Used for larger or tougher items -- whole chickens, ribs, pork shoulder, brisket. Lower heat, longer time.

Every good grill setup should have both zones available (two-zone fire).

## Charcoal vs gas

| Factor | Charcoal | Gas |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Flavor | Smokier, more complex | Cleaner, more neutral |
| Heat range | Very high to low (versatile) | Moderate to high |
| Startup time | 15-20 minutes | 5-10 minutes |
| Temperature control | Requires skill (vent management) | Dial adjustment |
| Cost per cook | Higher (fuel is consumed) | Lower (propane is cheaper per hour) |
| Cleanup | Ash disposal required | Minimal |

<Alert tone="info" title="Lump vs briquettes">
Lump charcoal is pure carbonized wood -- burns hotter, produces less ash, and has no additives. Briquettes are compressed with binders and burn more evenly and predictably. Both work well; lump is better for high-heat searing, briquettes for long steady cooks.
</Alert>

## Wood and smoke

Wood adds flavor that neither charcoal nor gas can produce alone. Common smoking woods:

| Wood | Flavor profile | Pairs with |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hickory | Strong, bacon-like | Pork, beef, poultry |
| Oak | Medium, balanced | Beef, brisket, sausage |
| Mesquite | Intense, earthy | Beef (use sparingly, can turn bitter) |
| Apple | Mild, sweet | Pork, poultry, fish |
| Cherry | Mild, fruity, adds color | Pork, poultry, duck |
| Pecan | Medium, nutty | Poultry, pork, baking |

For grilling, add wood chunks directly to charcoal or use a smoker box on gas. For smoking, wood is the primary or supplemental fuel source.

## Smoking basics

Smoking is low-and-slow cooking (225-275°F) with wood smoke over hours. It breaks down collagen in tough, connective-tissue-rich cuts, transforming them into tender, flavorful meat.

### Classic smoked items

- **Brisket** -- 10-14 hours at 225-250°F. The benchmark BBQ challenge.
- **Pork shoulder / butt** -- 8-12 hours. Pulled pork.
- **Ribs** (spare or baby back) -- 4-6 hours. The 3-2-1 method (3 hours smoke, 2 hours wrapped, 1 hour sauced) is a reliable starting framework.
- **Whole chicken** -- 2-3 hours at 275°F. Crisp skin with smoky flavor.

<Alert tone="warning" title="The stall">
Large cuts like brisket and pork shoulder hit a temperature plateau around 150-170°F that can last for hours. This is caused by evaporative cooling (moisture on the surface). Wrapping in butcher paper or foil ("the Texas crutch") pushes through the stall faster.
</Alert>

## Fire management

### Vent control (charcoal)

Charcoal grills regulate temperature through airflow:

- **Bottom vents (intake)** control oxygen supply. More open = hotter fire.
- **Top vent (exhaust)** should generally stay open so smoke flows and doesn't become stale.
- Closing both vents smothers the fire.

### Temperature zones

Arrange coals on one side for a hot zone and leave the other side empty for an indirect zone. This gives you a place to move food if it is cooking too fast or flaring up.

## Practical study approach

1. Master the two-zone fire setup -- it is the foundation of all good grilling.
2. Practice charcoal vent control to hold a target temperature (250°F and 400°F are good benchmarks).
3. Start smoking with pork shoulder -- it is forgiving and hard to ruin.
4. Use an instant-read thermometer for doneness and a grill-mounted probe for ambient temp.
5. Keep a cook log: fuel, temp, time, and results. This builds intuition faster than guessing.
