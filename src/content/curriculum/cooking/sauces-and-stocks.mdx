## Why sauces matter

Sauces transform simple cooked food into complete dishes. They add moisture, flavor, richness, and visual appeal. Most professional cooking is built on sauce technique.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Stocks | The foundation liquid for most sauces and soups |
| Mother sauces | Five classical bases from which hundreds of sauces derive |
| Pan sauces | Quick sauces built from fond (browned bits) after searing |
| Vinaigrettes | Emulsified or broken dressings for salads and finishing |
| Reduction | Concentrating flavors by evaporating liquid |

## Stocks

Stock is the backbone of sauce making. It is made by simmering bones, aromatics, and water for an extended period to extract flavor, collagen, and body.

| Stock type | Base | Simmer time | Uses |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Chicken stock | Chicken bones, carcasses | 4-6 hours | Soups, pan sauces, risotto, braises |
| Beef/veal stock | Roasted beef or veal bones | 8-12 hours | Rich sauces, demi-glace, French onion soup |
| Fish stock (fumet) | Fish bones and heads | 30-45 minutes | Seafood sauces, chowders, poaching liquid |
| Vegetable stock | Onion, carrot, celery, herbs | 45-60 minutes | Universal base, lighter dishes |

### Key principles

- **Cold start**: begin with cold water to extract proteins gradually and produce a clearer stock.
- **Skim regularly**: remove foam and impurities that rise to the surface.
- **Gentle simmer, never boil**: boiling emulsifies fat into the liquid, making it cloudy and greasy.
- **Strain, don't press**: pressing solids forces cloudy particles through.

<Alert tone="info" title="Gelatin test">
Good stock sets to a gel when refrigerated. That body comes from collagen extracted from bones and connective tissue. If your stock is watery, it needed more bones or more time.
</Alert>

## The five mother sauces

Classical French cuisine defines five base sauces from which nearly all others are derived:

| Mother sauce | Base | Thickener | Key derivatives |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Béchamel | Milk | Roux (butter + flour) | Mornay (cheese), cream sauce, soufflé base |
| Velouté | Light stock (chicken, fish) | Roux | Suprême (cream), Allemande (egg yolk) |
| Espagnole | Brown stock | Roux + tomato | Demi-glace, Bordelaise, Robert |
| Tomato sauce | Tomatoes + stock | Reduction (or roux) | Marinara, Creole, Portuguese |
| Hollandaise | Clarified butter | Egg yolk emulsion | Béarnaise, Maltaise, Mousseline |

### Roux

A roux is equal parts butter and flour cooked together. It thickens sauces while adding body and richness.

- **White roux** (1-2 min): for béchamel and velouté. Minimal color, maximum thickening.
- **Blond roux** (3-5 min): slightly nutty, used in velouté and cream soups.
- **Brown roux** (8-15 min): deep color and flavor, used in espagnole and gumbo. Thickens less because starch breaks down with cooking.

## Pan sauces

A pan sauce is built in the same pan used to sear meat. It is the fastest route to a restaurant-quality sauce at home.

### Process

1. **Sear** the protein and remove it to rest.
2. **Deglaze**: add liquid (wine, stock, vinegar) to the hot pan and scrape up the fond (browned bits stuck to the bottom). This is concentrated flavor.
3. **Reduce** the liquid by half or more to concentrate.
4. **Finish** with butter (mount with cold butter, swirling to emulsify), cream, mustard, or herbs.
5. **Season** and pour over the rested protein.

The entire process takes 3-5 minutes and uses no additional pots.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Deglaze safely">
Adding liquid to a very hot pan produces a burst of steam. Keep your face back and use a long-handled tool. If the pan has residual fat, wine or spirits can briefly flame -- this is normal but keep the pan away from overhead cabinets.
</Alert>

## Vinaigrettes

A vinaigrette is an emulsion of oil and acid (vinegar or citrus juice).

### Basic ratio

**3 parts oil to 1 part acid**, adjusted to taste. Add salt, pepper, and optionally mustard (which acts as an emulsifier and stabilizer).

### Technique

1. Combine acid, salt, pepper, and mustard (if using) in a bowl.
2. Whisk constantly while drizzling in oil in a thin stream.
3. The mustard and mechanical action create a temporary emulsion.
4. Taste and adjust. It should be balanced -- sharp but not harsh, rich but not oily.

## Reduction

Reduction is the simplest concentration technique: simmer a liquid until water evaporates, leaving behind intensified flavor and a thicker consistency.

- **Demi-glace**: espagnole reduced with brown stock until syrupy. One of the most prized sauces in classical cooking.
- **Balsamic reduction**: balsamic vinegar simmered until thick and sweet. Used as a finishing drizzle.
- **Wine reduction**: wine reduced with shallots and herbs, then finished with butter. The base of many French sauces.

## Practical study approach

1. Make a basic chicken stock from scratch -- it teaches patience, skimming, and flavor extraction.
2. Learn béchamel (the easiest mother sauce) and make mac and cheese from it.
3. Practice a pan sauce every time you sear meat -- deglaze, reduce, finish with butter.
4. Make a vinaigrette by hand to understand emulsion behavior.
5. Taste at every stage. Sauce making is about building and adjusting flavor incrementally.
