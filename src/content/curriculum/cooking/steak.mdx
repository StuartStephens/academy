## What matters most with steak

Steak quality depends on cut, thickness, temperature management, seasoning, surface dryness, and rest time after cooking. The cooking method shapes the crust, smoke flavor, and level of control you have over doneness.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Cut selection | Fat content and muscle structure change flavor and tenderness |
| Surface drying | Better browning needs less surface moisture |
| Searing | Develops crust and flavor through high heat |
| Internal doneness | Changes texture and juiciness significantly |
| Resting | Helps redistribute juices after cooking |

## Choosing a cut

| Cut | Location | Character | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Ribeye | Rib section | Well-marbled, rich, forgiving | Pan, grill, reverse sear |
| New York strip | Short loin | Firm, beefy, moderate fat | Pan, grill |
| Filet mignon | Tenderloin | Very tender, lean, mild flavor | Pan (with butter baste), reverse sear |
| T-bone / Porterhouse | Short loin | Two muscles (strip + tenderloin) | Grill, reverse sear |
| Flank / skirt | Belly / diaphragm | Lean, strong flavor, must slice thin against grain | Grill, open fire |

Thickness matters as much as cut. Steaks under 1 inch are hard to cook past rare without overcooking the exterior. Target 1-1.5 inches for the best balance of crust and interior control.

## Preparation (all methods)

1. **Dry the surface** -- pat thoroughly with paper towels. Moisture is the enemy of browning.
2. **Season generously** -- coarse salt and black pepper, applied 30-60 minutes before cooking (or just before). Early salting draws moisture out, which is then reabsorbed, seasoning deeper.
3. **Temper** -- let the steak sit at room temperature for 20-30 minutes before cooking for more even heat penetration.

<Alert tone="info" title="The Maillard reaction">
The brown crust on a steak is not "sealing in juices." It is the Maillard reaction -- amino acids and sugars reacting at high heat to create hundreds of flavor compounds. A dry surface and high heat are what make it happen.
</Alert>

## Method 1: Pan searing

The most controlled indoor method. Best for individual steaks, 1-1.5 inches thick.

### Equipment
- Heavy skillet (cast iron preferred for heat retention)
- High smoke-point oil (avocado, grapeseed, or clarified butter)
- Butter, garlic, thyme for basting (optional finish)

### Process

1. Heat the skillet over high heat until it just begins to smoke.
2. Add a thin layer of oil.
3. Place the steak away from you to avoid splatter. Do not move it.
4. Sear 3-4 minutes per side for a 1-inch steak targeting medium-rare.
5. **Optional butter baste**: in the last minute, add butter, crushed garlic, and thyme. Tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the steak repeatedly.
6. Remove at ~5°F below target temp (carryover cooking will finish it).
7. Rest 5-8 minutes on a cutting board.

## Method 2: Grill (charcoal or gas)

Grilling adds smoky flavor and handles thicker cuts or multiple steaks well.

### Setup

- **Two-zone fire**: one side high heat (direct), one side low/no heat (indirect). This lets you sear over flame and move to the cooler side if the exterior is done before the interior.
- Clean grates and oil them lightly before cooking.

### Process

1. Place steaks over direct high heat.
2. Sear 2-3 minutes per side with the lid open.
3. Move to indirect heat, close the lid, and cook to target internal temp.
4. For thin steaks (under 1 inch), skip indirect -- sear both sides and pull.
5. Rest 5-8 minutes.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Flare-ups">
Fat dripping onto coals or burners causes flare-ups that char the surface and deposit bitter soot. Move the steak to the cool zone if flames engulf it. Trimming excess external fat before grilling helps.
</Alert>

## Method 3: Reverse sear

The most reliable method for thick steaks (1.5 inches or more). You cook low first, then sear at the end.

### Process

1. Place the steak on a wire rack over a sheet pan in a 225-275°F oven.
2. Cook until internal temperature reaches ~10-15°F below your target (typically 115°F for medium-rare final).
3. Remove and rest 5 minutes while you heat a cast iron skillet to smoking hot.
4. Sear 45-60 seconds per side in a ripping hot pan with oil.
5. The result is an edge-to-edge even pink interior with a deeply browned crust.

The reverse sear works because slow, gentle heat gives you a wide window to hit the right internal temp. The final sear is purely for crust -- the interior is already done.

## Method 4: Open fire / campfire

Cooking steak over an open flame is the oldest method. It produces excellent results with attention to fire management.

### Fire setup

- Build a hardwood fire (oak, hickory, mesquite) and let it burn down to hot coals with some active flame. You want radiant heat from coals, not direct flame.
- Use a grill grate over the fire pit, or cook directly on coals (for thicker cuts wrapped in foil or placed on a clean coal bed).

### Process

1. Season the steak as normal.
2. Place on the grate 4-6 inches above the coals.
3. Sear 3-4 minutes per side, adjusting height to manage heat.
4. If cooking directly on coals (dirty steak method): use lump charcoal or hardwood coals, blow off ash, and place the steak directly on the embers for 2-3 minutes per side. The crust is extraordinary.
5. Rest 5-8 minutes.

<Alert tone="info" title="Fire reading">
Hold your hand 6 inches above the coals. If you can hold it for 2-3 seconds, it is high heat (good for searing). 4-5 seconds is medium. If the coals are cooler than that, add more fuel and wait.
</Alert>

## Doneness reference

| Level | Internal temp (pull) | Internal temp (after rest) | Feel |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rare | 115°F / 46°C | 120°F / 49°C | Cool red center, very soft |
| Medium-rare | 125°F / 52°C | 130°F / 54°C | Warm red center, yielding |
| Medium | 135°F / 57°C | 140°F / 60°C | Warm pink center, firmer |
| Medium-well | 145°F / 63°C | 150°F / 66°C | Slight pink, stiff |
| Well done | 155°F+ / 68°C+ | 160°F+ / 71°C+ | No pink, firm throughout |

An instant-read thermometer is the most reliable doneness tool. The touch test is a rough guide but not accurate enough for consistent results.

## Practical study approach

1. Start with pan searing -- it offers the most control and feedback.
2. Use an instant-read thermometer until you develop intuition for doneness.
3. Try the reverse sear on a thick (1.5"+) ribeye to see the difference edge-to-edge evenness makes.
4. Experiment with open fire once you are comfortable managing heat -- it rewards fire-reading skill.
5. Always rest the steak. Cutting too early loses juice and flavor.
