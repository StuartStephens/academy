## Why knife skills matter

The knife is the most-used tool in any kitchen. Good technique improves speed, consistency, safety, and the final texture of food. Even cooking depends on even cuts.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Knife types | Different tasks require different blade shapes |
| Grip and posture | Proper hold prevents fatigue and injury |
| Common cuts | Uniform pieces cook evenly and present well |
| Sharpening | A sharp knife is safer and more effective than a dull one |
| Board technique | The cutting surface and hand position affect everything |

## Essential knives

You need far fewer knives than most sets suggest. Three will cover nearly everything:

| Knife | Blade length | Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chef's knife | 8-10 inches | Primary all-purpose knife -- chopping, slicing, mincing, dicing |
| Paring knife | 3-4 inches | Detail work -- peeling, trimming, small cuts |
| Serrated / bread knife | 8-10 inches | Slicing bread, tomatoes, and delicate items without crushing |

A flexible boning knife is useful if you break down meat. A cleaver is helpful for dense root vegetables and bone-in cuts. Everything else is optional.

## Grip

### The pinch grip

The correct chef's knife grip is the **pinch grip**:

1. Pinch the blade just ahead of the heel (where blade meets handle) between your thumb and the side of your index finger.
2. Wrap your remaining three fingers around the handle.
3. Your thumb and index finger should be on the blade itself, not the handle.

This grip gives maximum control, balance, and precision. It feels odd at first if you are used to gripping the handle like a hammer, but it is universally taught in professional kitchens.

### The guide hand (claw)

Your non-knife hand holds the food and guides the cut:

1. Curl your fingertips under, so your knuckles protrude forward.
2. Rest the flat side of the blade against your knuckles -- they act as a guide rail.
3. Never let fingertips extend past your knuckles.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Most common kitchen injury">
Knife cuts are the most frequent kitchen injury. Almost all of them happen because of a dull blade (requiring excessive force) or exposed fingertips (no claw grip). Both are preventable.
</Alert>

## Common cuts

| Cut | Size | Shape | Used for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rough chop | Irregular, ~1 inch | Any | Stocks, stews, rustic dishes |
| Dice (large) | 3/4 inch cubes | Uniform | Soups, roasted vegetables |
| Dice (medium) | 1/2 inch cubes | Uniform | Sautés, salsas, fillings |
| Dice (small / brunoise) | 1/4 inch cubes | Uniform | Garnishes, fine sauces |
| Julienne | 1/8 x 1/8 x 2 inches | Matchstick | Stir-fry, salads, garnish |
| Chiffonade | Thin ribbons | Rolled and sliced | Herbs, leafy greens |
| Mince | As fine as possible | Irregular tiny pieces | Garlic, ginger, shallots |
| Slice | Varies | Flat rounds or half-moons | Onions, tomatoes, potatoes |

### How to dice an onion

1. Cut the onion in half through the root, peel.
2. Place cut-side down. Make horizontal cuts toward (but not through) the root.
3. Make vertical cuts from stem to root.
4. Slice across to produce even dice. The root holds everything together until the last cuts.

This single technique is used daily in professional kitchens and demonstrates how a structured approach produces uniform results.

## Sharpening and maintenance

### Honing vs sharpening

- **Honing** (with a honing steel) realigns the edge of a blade that has rolled or bent with use. Do this regularly -- before every session or every few uses.
- **Sharpening** (with a whetstone or professional service) removes metal to create a new edge. Needed when honing no longer restores performance, typically every few months.

### Whetstone basics

1. Soak the stone in water for 5-10 minutes (if water stone).
2. Hold the blade at a 15-20° angle to the stone.
3. Draw the blade across the stone, alternating sides, maintaining consistent angle.
4. Start with a coarser grit (1000) and finish with a finer grit (3000-6000).

<Alert tone="info" title="The sharp knife paradox">
A sharp knife is safer than a dull one. Dull knives slip off food surfaces unpredictably and require more force, making injuries worse when they happen. A sharp knife goes where you point it.
</Alert>

## Board technique

- Use a large, stable cutting board. Small boards crowd your work and increase slipping risk.
- Place a damp towel under the board to prevent sliding.
- Wood and plastic are both acceptable. Wood is easier on edges; plastic is easier to sanitize.
- Use separate boards for raw meat and ready-to-eat food, or sanitize thoroughly between uses.

## Practical study approach

1. Learn the pinch grip and claw hand -- practice until they feel natural.
2. Dice 5 onions using the structured method above. Consistency improves rapidly with repetition.
3. Practice julienne on carrots -- the uniform size forces discipline.
4. Learn to hone your knife before each session. It takes 30 seconds and maintains the edge.
5. Sharpen on a 1000/3000 whetstone once you notice honing is no longer enough.
