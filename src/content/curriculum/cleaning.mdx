## Why cleaning matters

Cleaning supports health, safety, comfort, and maintenance. Good cleaning reduces dust, residue, odors, contamination risk, and wear on surfaces and equipment.

## Core principles

| Principle | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Work top to bottom | Prevents re-soiling lower surfaces |
| Clean before disinfecting | Dirt and residue can reduce disinfectant effectiveness |
| Use the right product | Different surfaces and soils require different methods |
| Separate tools by area | Reduces cross-contamination |
| Follow a repeatable routine | Improves quality and saves time |

## Common cleaning categories

1. **Routine cleaning**: dusting, vacuuming, wiping surfaces, trash removal.
2. **Deep cleaning**: behind appliances, grout, vents, neglected buildup.
3. **Sanitizing/disinfecting**: higher-touch areas and contamination control.
4. **Specialized cleaning**: glass, carpets, kitchens, bathrooms, equipment.

## Basic workflow

1. Gather tools and products.
2. Remove clutter and dry debris first.
3. Clean high surfaces and frequently touched areas.
4. Handle kitchens and bathrooms with appropriate chemical/process separation.
5. Finish floors last.

## Tools and materials

- Microfiber cloths
- Mop and bucket or flat mop system
- Vacuum
- Scrub brushes and sponges
- Surface-specific cleaners
- Gloves and other protective equipment when needed

<Alert tone="warning" title="Safety reminder">
Never mix cleaning chemicals unless the product guidance explicitly allows it. Some combinations can release dangerous gases or damage surfaces.
</Alert>

## What good cleaning includes

- Surface compatibility
- Ventilation awareness
- Label reading and dilution accuracy
- Consistent routines for kitchens, bathrooms, and shared spaces
- Periodic deep-clean scheduling instead of only reactive cleaning
