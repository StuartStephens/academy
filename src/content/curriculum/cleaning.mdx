## Why cleaning matters

Cleaning supports health, safety, comfort, and maintenance. Good cleaning reduces dust, residue, odors, contamination risk, and wear on surfaces and equipment.

## Core principles

| Principle | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Work top to bottom | Prevents re-soiling lower surfaces |
| Clean before disinfecting | Dirt and residue can reduce disinfectant effectiveness |
| Use the right product | Different surfaces and soils require different methods |
| Separate tools by area | Reduces cross-contamination |
| Follow a repeatable routine | Improves quality and saves time |

## Common cleaning categories

1. **Routine cleaning**: dusting, vacuuming, wiping surfaces, trash removal.
2. **Deep cleaning**: behind appliances, grout, vents, neglected buildup.
3. **Sanitizing/disinfecting**: higher-touch areas and contamination control.
4. **Specialized cleaning**: glass, carpets, kitchens, bathrooms, equipment.

## Basic workflow

1. Gather tools and products.
2. Remove clutter and dry debris first.
3. Clean high surfaces and frequently touched areas.
4. Handle kitchens and bathrooms with appropriate chemical/process separation.
5. Finish floors last.

## Tools and materials

- Microfiber cloths
- Mop and bucket or flat mop system
- Vacuum
- Scrub brushes and sponges
- Surface-specific cleaners
- Gloves and other protective equipment when needed

## Clean vs disinfect

Cleaning removes soil, dust, oils, and residue. Disinfecting is a separate step meant to reduce certain microorganisms on suitable surfaces.

You often need to clean first, then disinfect if disinfection is actually needed.

## How to clean without damaging the surface

The safest default process is:

1. Remove loose dust and grit first.
2. Use the mildest cleaner that will do the job.
3. Put cleaner on the cloth when possible, not directly on the object.
4. Avoid soaking seams, ports, joints, finishes, or adhesives.
5. Dry the surface after cleaning if moisture can sit or wick inward.

## Example: cleaning an MX Master 3 mouse safely

An MX Master 3 or similar mouse should be cleaned gently because it has switches, seams, rubberized surfaces, a scroll wheel assembly, and a charging/data port.

### Safe approach

1. Turn the mouse off and disconnect it.
2. Use a dry microfiber cloth to remove dust and skin debris.
3. Lightly dampen a microfiber cloth with water or a mild electronics-safe cleaner.
4. Wipe the shell, buttons, and thumb area without dripping moisture into seams.
5. Use a dry cotton swab or soft brush around the wheel and creases.
6. Dry it fully before use.

### Avoid

- Spraying cleaner directly onto the mouse.
- Soaking the scroll wheel area or USB port.
- Using harsh solvents on rubberized surfaces.
- Using abrasive pads or paper towels that can scratch or wear coatings.

## Example: cleaning a car properly

Cars have multiple materials at once: paint, glass, trim, rubber, fabric, leather, screens, and metal. The wrong product or technique can damage finishes quickly.

### Safer car-cleaning order

1. Rinse away loose grit first.
2. Wash with an automotive soap, not dish soap.
3. Use separate wash tools for paint, wheels, and dirty lower areas.
4. Dry with clean microfiber towels.
5. Use dedicated cleaners for glass, interior plastics, leather, or screens.

### Avoid

- Scrubbing dry dirt into paint.
- Using harsh household degreasers on interior plastics or trim.
- Using ammonia-heavy cleaners on tinted surfaces.
- Reusing dirty cloths that grind debris into the finish.

## Example: cleaning glassware

Glassware often looks simple but can be damaged by thermal shock, residue, hard-water spotting, or abrasive handling.

### Safe approach

- Wash with warm water and a mild soap.
- Use soft sponges or cloths rather than abrasive scrubbers.
- Rinse thoroughly to reduce spotting.
- Dry with a lint-free towel if clarity matters.

### Avoid

- Sudden extreme temperature changes.
- Abrasives on delicate or coated glass.
- Letting mineral-heavy water air-dry when appearance matters.

## Example: cleaning countertops

Countertops vary widely. Stone, laminate, butcher block, quartz, stainless, and tile all behave differently.

### General safer rule

Use a cleaner matched to the countertop material, then wipe dry.

### Common cautions

- Natural stone can be damaged by acidic cleaners.
- Wood counters dislike standing water.
- Some disinfectants can dull finishes if used too aggressively or too often.
- Abrasives can scratch polished surfaces.

## Are Clorox wipes toxic?

Disinfecting wipes are useful for some surfaces, but they are not an all-purpose default cleaner.

### Practical answer

- They can irritate skin, eyes, and lungs, especially with heavy use or poor ventilation.
- They should not be treated as harmless simply because they are common household products.
- They are generally not intended for every material or every object.

### Important cautions

- Follow the label for surface compatibility and contact time.
- Keep them away from food-contact use unless the label supports that use and any required rinse step is followed.
- Do not use them casually on sensitive electronics, unfinished wood, or delicate coatings.
- Do not mix them with other chemicals.

## How to think about the proper way to clean something

Before cleaning any object, ask:

1. What material is it made from?
2. Is the surface sealed, coated, painted, porous, or electronic?
3. Is the goal dust removal, stain removal, degreasing, or disinfection?
4. Could liquid, abrasion, heat, or chemicals damage it?

The proper way is usually the least aggressive method that reaches the cleaning goal.

## What good cleaning includes

- Surface compatibility
- Ventilation awareness
- Label reading and dilution accuracy
- Consistent routines for kitchens, bathrooms, and shared spaces
- Periodic deep-clean scheduling instead of only reactive cleaning
