## Why oil matters

Oil is central to transportation, industrial feedstocks, geopolitics, and global energy systems. It affects fuel supply, plastics, chemicals, trade routes, and strategic planning.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Extraction | Production methods shape cost and supply |
| Refining | Crude oil must be processed into useful products |
| Transportation | Pipelines, tankers, and chokepoints affect global flows |
| Industrial use | Oil supports fuels, lubricants, plastics, and chemicals |
| Strategic geography | Oil routes and reserves influence geopolitics |

## What oil is

Petroleum (crude oil) is a naturally occurring mixture of hydrocarbons formed from ancient organic material buried under heat and pressure over millions of years. It is found in porous rock formations, typically with natural gas.

Crude oil varies widely in quality. **Light sweet crude** (low density, low sulfur) is easier and cheaper to refine. **Heavy sour crude** (high density, high sulfur) requires more processing and yields different product ratios.

## Extraction

### Conventional drilling

A well is drilled into a reservoir and oil flows to the surface driven by natural pressure, then by pumps as pressure declines. Conventional fields are the cheapest to produce but are increasingly mature.

### Offshore drilling

Platforms and subsea systems extract oil from beneath the ocean floor. Deepwater drilling is technically complex and expensive, but accesses large reserves (Gulf of Mexico, North Sea, offshore Brazil).

### Unconventional methods

- **Hydraulic fracturing (fracking)** -- injects high-pressure fluid into shale rock to release trapped oil and gas. Transformed US production after 2008.
- **Oil sands** -- heavy crude mixed with sand and clay, extracted by mining or steam injection. Concentrated in Alberta, Canada.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Environmental cost">
All extraction methods carry environmental risk: habitat disruption, water contamination, methane emissions, and spill potential. Unconventional methods typically have a larger surface footprint and higher energy input per barrel.
</Alert>

## Refining

Crude oil is useless as extracted. Refineries separate it into products through distillation and chemical processing.

| Product | Fraction | Primary use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) | Lightest | Heating, cooking fuel |
| Gasoline | Light | Vehicle fuel |
| Kerosene / jet fuel | Medium | Aviation, heating |
| Diesel | Medium | Trucks, ships, generators |
| Fuel oil | Heavy | Shipping, power plants |
| Asphalt | Heaviest | Road paving |
| Petrochemical feedstocks | Various | Plastics, chemicals, synthetic materials |

A single barrel of crude (42 US gallons) yields roughly 19-20 gallons of gasoline, 11-12 gallons of diesel and heating oil, and the remainder as other products. Nothing is wasted -- refineries optimize to sell every fraction.

## Transportation and chokepoints

Oil moves by pipeline, tanker ship, rail, and truck. The global supply chain depends on a few critical transit points.

| Chokepoint | Location | Daily flow (approx.) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strait of Hormuz | Persian Gulf | ~21 million barrels/day |
| Strait of Malacca | Southeast Asia | ~16 million barrels/day |
| Suez Canal | Egypt | ~5 million barrels/day |
| Bab el-Mandeb | Red Sea/Yemen | ~5 million barrels/day |
| Panama Canal | Central America | ~1 million barrels/day |

Disruption at any of these points -- through conflict, piracy, or accident -- can spike global oil prices within hours.

<Alert tone="info" title="Strategic reserves">
Major consuming nations maintain strategic petroleum reserves (the US SPR holds ~350+ million barrels) to buffer supply shocks. These are emergency stockpiles, not normal supply.
</Alert>

## Geopolitics of oil

Oil wealth shapes international relations, alliances, and conflicts.

- **OPEC and OPEC+** coordinate production among major exporters to influence price. Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, UAE, and others are key members.
- **Petrostates** -- countries whose economies and governments depend heavily on oil revenue -- are vulnerable to price swings and often have governance challenges.
- **Energy independence** became a US policy goal after the 1973 Arab oil embargo. The shale revolution brought the US closer to self-sufficiency by the 2010s.
- **Energy transition** -- as the world shifts toward renewables and electrification, oil-dependent economies face long-term structural risk.

## Everyday products from oil

Beyond fuel, petroleum is the feedstock for thousands of products:

- Plastics (polyethylene, polypropylene, PVC)
- Synthetic fibers (polyester, nylon)
- Pharmaceuticals and medical devices
- Fertilizers (via natural gas, a co-product)
- Lubricants, waxes, and solvents
- Asphalt for roads

## Practical study approach

1. Learn the upstream/midstream/downstream framework as an organizing principle.
2. Understand why crude oil quality (light vs heavy, sweet vs sour) matters for refining economics.
3. Study the map of global chokepoints and which consuming regions depend on each.
4. Follow oil price movements for a month to see how geopolitics, weather, and demand interact.
5. Examine the tension between oil as an essential economic input and oil as a driver of climate change.
