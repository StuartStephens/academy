## What makes cigars distinct?

Cigars are crafted tobacco products built from filler, binder, and wrapper leaves. Their quality depends on tobacco variety, curing, fermentation, rolling, aging, and storage conditions.

## Core topics

| Topic | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Tobacco leaf roles | Wrapper, binder, and filler each affect flavor and performance |
| Curing and fermentation | These stages strongly shape aroma and character |
| Rolling and construction | Good draw and burn depend on correct assembly |
| Humidity and storage | Improper storage can ruin product quality |
| Origin and blend | Region and blending choices affect flavor profile |

## Cigar anatomy

A cigar has three layers, each serving a different purpose:

- **Filler** -- the core tobacco that makes up the bulk of the cigar. Can be long filler (whole leaves running the length of the cigar) or short filler (chopped leaf, found in machine-made cigars). Filler is the primary source of flavor and body.
- **Binder** -- a tough, elastic leaf wrapped around the filler to hold its shape. Binder contributes some flavor but is chosen primarily for structural integrity.
- **Wrapper** -- the outermost leaf, selected for appearance, texture, and aroma. The wrapper is the most expensive component and contributes significantly to flavor because it is in direct contact with the smoker's palate.

### Wrapper color and types

| Wrapper shade | Name | Character |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Light tan | Claro / Connecticut | Mild, creamy, smooth |
| Medium brown | Natural / Colorado | Balanced, medium body |
| Dark brown | Maduro | Rich, sweet, full-bodied |
| Very dark / black | Oscuro | Intense, bold, oily |

Darker wrappers have typically undergone longer fermentation, which breaks down sugars and produces richer flavors.

## From leaf to cigar

### Growing and harvesting

Tobacco plants are harvested leaf by leaf from the bottom up over several weeks. Lower leaves (volado) are milder; upper leaves (ligero) are stronger and oilier because they receive more sun.

### Curing

Fresh leaves are hung in curing barns for 4-8 weeks. During curing, chlorophyll breaks down and the leaf changes from green to brown. The method (air curing for most cigar tobacco) affects the final chemistry of the leaf.

### Fermentation

After curing, leaves are stacked in large piles (pilones) where internal heat and moisture trigger fermentation. This process:

- Reduces harshness and ammonia
- Develops complex flavors
- Stabilizes the leaf for long-term use

Premium tobacco may be fermented for months or even years. Temperature is monitored carefully -- if a pilon gets too hot, the tobacco can be damaged.

### Rolling

Skilled rollers (torcedores) blend filler leaves, wrap them in a binder, then apply the wrapper. Good construction requires:

- Even filler distribution for consistent draw
- Correct tension so the cigar does not unravel or plug
- A smooth, seamless wrapper application
- A properly applied cap that holds together when cut

<Alert tone="info" title="Hand-rolled vs machine-made">
Hand-rolled cigars use long filler and are assembled by skilled workers. They are more expensive but offer better draw, more complex flavor, and more consistent burn. Machine-made cigars use short filler and homogenized binder, trading quality for scale and lower cost.
</Alert>

## Storage and humidity

Cigars are hygroscopic -- they absorb and release moisture from their environment. Proper storage is essential.

- **Ideal humidity**: 65-72% relative humidity (RH). Too dry and the cigar cracks, burns hot, and tastes harsh. Too wet and it becomes plugged, moldy, or prone to beetle infestation.
- **Ideal temperature**: 65-70°F (18-21°C). Higher temperatures accelerate beetle hatching and mold growth.
- **Humidors** maintain stable conditions using a sealed box with a humidification device and hygrometer.

<Alert tone="warning" title="Tobacco beetles">
Tobacco beetles (Lasioderma serricorne) can infest stored cigars, boring holes through them and ruining entire collections. They hatch above ~72°F. Keep storage cool and inspect regularly.
</Alert>

## Cutting, lighting, and smoking

### Cutting

The cap must be cut to allow draw. Methods include:

- **Straight cut** (guillotine) -- clean, full draw, most common.
- **V-cut** -- wedge-shaped notch, concentrates smoke.
- **Punch cut** -- small circular hole, restricts draw slightly, tidy appearance.

### Lighting

- Use a butane lighter or cedar spill. Avoid petrol lighters and candles, which can impart off-flavors.
- Toast the foot (the open end) before drawing, rotating to get an even initial burn.
- Do not inhale cigar smoke into the lungs -- cigar smoking is palate-based.

## Major producing regions

| Region | Known for |
| --- | --- |
| Cuba | Full-bodied, earthy, complex. The historical benchmark. |
| Nicaragua | Rich, spicy, diverse growing regions (Estelí, Jalapa, Condega) |
| Dominican Republic | Smooth, balanced, wide range from mild to medium-full |
| Honduras | Medium to full body, often used in value-oriented blends |
| Connecticut (US) | Shade-grown wrapper leaf, mild and creamy |

## Practical study approach

1. Learn the three-part anatomy (filler, binder, wrapper) and how each contributes to the smoke.
2. Understand curing and fermentation as the processes that distinguish premium tobacco from raw leaf.
3. Practice proper storage -- a small humidor with a digital hygrometer is a minimal setup.
4. Try cigars from at least three different regions to calibrate your palate.
5. Pay attention to construction: draw resistance, burn evenness, and ash quality reveal rolling skill.
