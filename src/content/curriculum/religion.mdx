## Why study religion?

Religion shapes ethics, law, art, identity, ritual, community life, and political history. Studying religion comparatively helps explain how people organize meaning, authority, and moral life across societies.

## What this section covers

| Tradition | Core focus |
| --- | --- |
| Judaism | Covenant, scripture, law, and Jewish history |
| Christianity | Jesus, church traditions, doctrine, and global expansion |
| Islam | Revelation, law, practice, and global Muslim communities |
| Hinduism | Diverse traditions, ritual life, philosophy, and social practice |
| Buddhism | Suffering, practice, ethics, and liberation traditions |

## Study approach

1. Learn each tradition in its own terms before comparing it to others.
2. Distinguish beliefs, rituals, texts, institutions, and historical development.
3. Avoid reducing religions to stereotypes or single political events.
4. Notice internal diversity within every tradition.

<Alert tone="info" title="Comparative study tip">
Religions are not only belief systems. They are also lived traditions with rituals, institutions, law, memory, and cultural variation.
</Alert>
