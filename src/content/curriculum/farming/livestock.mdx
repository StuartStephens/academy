## Why livestock systems differ

Livestock farming is about much more than the animal itself. Land, feed, housing, water, waste handling, health, and processing infrastructure all shape what is realistic and sustainable.

## Common livestock types

| Animal | Main farming considerations |
| --- | --- |
| Cows | Land, feed, fencing, health management, long production cycle |
| Chickens | Housing, predators, feed efficiency, disease management |
| Ducks | Water access, muddy ground management, housing, eggs/meat goals |
| Goats | Browsing behavior, fencing, parasite management, land fit |
| Sheep | Grazing systems, wool/meat split, predator pressure |
| Pigs | Feed conversion, housing, waste, strong fencing needs |

## Your red-meat question

Yes, other red-meat animals worth farming can include:

- **Goats**: useful in many climates and land conditions.
- **Sheep**: common for both meat and fiber systems.
- **Pigs**: not "red meat" in every casual sense people use, but often farmed for meat production economics.
- In some regions: **bison**, **deer**, or other specialty livestock, though those are more regulated and system-specific.

## Cows

- Require significant land, feed planning, and infrastructure.
- Can be raised for beef, dairy, or mixed farm systems.
- Water, fencing, and health monitoring are non-negotiable.

## Chickens

- Flexible for small and large farms.
- Can be focused on eggs, meat, or both depending on system.
- Predator management and sanitation matter a lot.

## Ducks

- Often hardier in wet conditions than chickens.
- Still require clean housing, feed management, and biosecurity.
- Water use and mud management need planning.

## Main decision questions

1. How much land and shelter do you actually have?
2. What feed sources are realistic?
3. Is the goal meat, eggs, dairy, breeding stock, or mixed production?
4. What veterinary and processing support exists locally?
